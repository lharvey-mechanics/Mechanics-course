Key terms

Pneumatics

Systems that use compressed air or gas to do work.

In the shop: An air impact wrench powered by shop air.

Hydraulics

Systems that use pressurized fluid to do work.

In the shop: A hydraulic jack lifting a vehicle.

Pressure

Force applied over an area (often PSI or kPa).

In the shop: Reading line pressure on a gauge.

Flow

How much fluid/air moves per time.

In the shop: A slow cylinder may be a flow restriction.

Force

A push or pull produced by pressure acting on an area.

In the shop: Calculating lift force in a hydraulic cylinder.

Pascal’s Law

Pressure in a confined fluid is transmitted equally in all directions.

In the shop: Explaining why jacks can lift heavy loads.

Compressible

Can be squeezed smaller (air).

In the shop: Air makes pneumatic systems “springy.”

Incompressible

Hard to squeeze (most hydraulic fluids).

In the shop: Hydraulics provide steady, strong force.

Reservoir

Container that stores hydraulic fluid.

In the shop: Checking fluid level before operating a lift.

Pump

Moves fluid to create flow (and pressure under load).

In the shop: Pumping a jack handle to raise a vehicle.

Compressor

Device that pressurizes air for pneumatic tools.

In the shop: Compressor supplying air to the shop line.

Control valve

Directs fluid/air to control direction, speed, or pressure.

In the shop: A valve controlling extend/retract on a cylinder.

Actuator (cylinder)

Component that turns pressure into movement.

In the shop: Air cylinder opening/closing a clamp.

Regulator

Reduces and controls air pressure to a set value.

In the shop: Setting line pressure for an air tool.

FRL unit

Filter–Regulator–Lubricator used for shop air systems.

In the shop: Filtering water and setting pressure at a station.

Hose and fitting

Lines and connectors carrying air/fluid under pressure.

In the shop: Inspecting a hose for cracks before use.

Bleeding

Removing trapped air from a hydraulic system.

In the shop: Bleeding a hydraulic jack that feels spongy.

Stored energy hazard

Pressurized systems can release energy suddenly.

In the shop: Safely depressurizing before disconnecting a line.
